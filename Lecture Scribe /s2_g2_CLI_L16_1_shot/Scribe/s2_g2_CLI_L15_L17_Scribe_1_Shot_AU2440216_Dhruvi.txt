\section{Definitions and Notation}

\subsection*{Joint Random Variables}

In science and real life, we are often interested in two or more random variables
at the same time.

Examples mentioned:

\begin{itemize}
\item Height and weight of giraffes
\item IQ and birthweight of children
\item Frequency of exercise and rate of heart disease
\item Air pollution level and respiratory illness rate
\item Number of Facebook friends and age of members
\end{itemize}

In such situations the random variables have a joint distribution that allows
computation of probabilities involving both variables and helps understand
relationships between variables.

If variables are not independent, covariance and correlation are used as
measures of dependence.

\subsection*{Discrete Case – Joint PMF}

Suppose $X$ and $Y$ are discrete random variables.

\[
X \in \{x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n\}
\]

\[
Y \in \{y_1,y_2,\dots,y_m\}
\]

The ordered pair $(X,Y)$ takes values in

\[
\{(x_1,y_1),(x_1,y_2),\dots,(x_n,y_m)\}
\]

\textbf{Definition (Joint PMF)}

\[
p(x_i,y_j)=P(X=x_i,Y=y_j)
\]

This gives the probability of the joint outcome occurring simultaneously.

\subsection*{Joint PMF Table Representation}

The joint PMF can be organized in a table with rows representing values of $X$
and columns representing values of $Y$.

\textbf{Key Properties}

\[
0 \le p(x,y) \le 1
\]

\[
\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^{m} p(x_i,y_j)=1
\]

\section{Continuous Case – Joint PDF}

The continuous case is analogous to the discrete case:

\begin{itemize}
\item Discrete values $\rightarrow$ Continuous intervals
\item Joint PMF $\rightarrow$ Joint PDF
\item Sums $\rightarrow$ Double integrals
\end{itemize}

If

\[
X \in [a,b], \quad Y \in [c,d]
\]

then the pair $(X,Y)$ takes values in

\[
[a,b] \times [c,d]
\]

\textbf{Definition (Joint PDF)}

A function $f(x,y)$ is called the joint probability density function of $X$ and
$Y$ if

\[
P((X,Y) \text{ lies in a small rectangle of area } dx\,dy)=f(x,y)\,dx\,dy
\]

\textbf{Key Properties}

\[
f(x,y) \ge 0
\]

\[
\int\int f(x,y)\,dx\,dy =1
\]

Note: The joint PDF $f(x,y)$ may take values greater than 1 since it is a
probability density and not a probability.

\section{Joint Cumulative Distribution Function (Joint CDF)}

Suppose $X$ and $Y$ are jointly distributed random variables.

\[
F(x,y)=P(X<x,Y<y)
\]

\section{Main Results}

\subsection*{Continuous Case}

If $X$ and $Y$ are continuous random variables with joint density $f(x,y)$ over
$[a,b]\times[c,d]$, then

\[
F(x,y)=\int_{c}^{y}\int_{a}^{x} f(u,v)\,du\,dv
\]

\subsection*{Recovering the Joint PDF}

\[
f(x,y)=\frac{\partial^2 F(x,y)}{\partial x \partial y}
\]

\subsection*{Discrete Case}

If $X$ and $Y$ are discrete random variables with joint PMF $p(x_i,y_j)$,

\[
F(x,y)=\sum_{x_i<x}\sum_{y_j<y} p(x_i,y_j)
\]

Interpretation:

\begin{itemize}
\item Add all probabilities in the table
\item For rows with $x_i<x$
\item For columns with $y_j<y$
\end{itemize}

\section{Proofs or Derivations}

\subsection*{Validity of Joint PDF (Normalization)}

Given

\[
f(x,y)=4xy, \quad 0<x<1, 0<y<1
\]

Check normalization:

\[
\int_0^1 \int_0^1 4xy\,dx\,dy
\]

\[
= \int_0^1 \left(\int_0^1 4xy\,dx \right) dy
\]

\[
= \int_0^1 2y\,dy
\]

\[
= [y^2]_0^1=1
\]

Thus $f(x,y)\ge0$ and total probability equals 1.

Therefore $f(x,y)$ is a valid joint PDF.

\section{Examples}

\subsection*{Example 1: Rolling Two Dice}

Experiment: Roll two fair dice.

Let

\[
X = \text{value on the first die}
\]

\[
T = \text{total (sum) of both dice}
\]

Each outcome $(i,j)$ has probability

\[
\frac{1}{36}
\]

Construct the joint probability table of $(X,T)$.

Observation:

Entries are either $0$ or $\frac{1}{36}$.

$X$ and $T$ are not independent.

\subsection*{Example 2: Continuous Joint PDF}

Given

\[
f(x,y)=4xy, \quad 0<x<1,0<y<1
\]

Tasks:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Show that $f(x,y)$ is a valid joint PDF
\item Visualize the event
\[
A=\{X<0.5, Y>0.5\}
\]
\item Compute
\[
P(A)
\]
\end{enumerate}

Joint support:

\[
(X,Y)\in [0,1]^2
\]

Probability calculation:

\[
P(A)=\int_{0}^{0.5}\int_{0.5}^{1}4xy\,dy\,dx
\]

\[
= \int_{0}^{0.5}[2xy^2]_{0.5}^{1}dx
\]

\[
= \int_{0}^{0.5}2x(1-0.25)\,dx
\]

\[
= \int_{0}^{0.5}\frac{3}{2}x\,dx
\]

\[
= \frac{3}{16}
\]

\section{Properties of the Joint CDF}

The joint CDF $F(x,y)$ must satisfy:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Non-decreasing property

If $x$ or $y$ increases, then $F(x,y)$ must stay constant or increase.

\item Lower-left property

\[
F(x,y)=0
\]

at the lower-left of the joint range.

\item Upper-right property

\[
\lim_{(x,y)\to(\infty,\infty)}F(x,y)=1
\]

\end{enumerate}
